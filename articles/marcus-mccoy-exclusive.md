My dad and my grandma have visited us here a couple of times when I was really young, but not in recent years. I still text with him and my grandma sometimes; the contact isn't constant, but it has grown as I've gotten older.

Navigating the Danish National Team Pathway
Josh: You’ve done well to progress through the ranks with Denmark, playing for the U18s, U19s, and now getting some time with the U20s. What is the vibe like in that setup? Is there a clear pathway to the senior team, and how competitive is that national pool?
Marcus Mccoy: The youth teams—from the U16s up through the U19s—are highly competitive. Denmark has a lot of great youth clubs and excellent young players, so there is always a lot of intense competition just to make those squads.
For me, I was a player who came into the national setup a bit late. I was involved a couple of times, but I wasn't the first name on the team sheet or the squad list. Because of that, being there was really a privilege for me. I know a lot of guys who were fixtures from the very start, but navigating the national team pathway is tough. Some of those players who were locks early on are no longer with the U21s because they either burned out at the club level or decided they didn't want to play football anymore.
The real shift happens when you reach the U21 level, because the U21s are solely senior professional players. You see guys playing at levels like Frankfurt and Leipzig, and that is the standard you need to reach the U21s.
Josh: It seems like a massive jump in terms of standard and environment.
Marcus Mccoy: It really is. The gap between the U19s and the U21s is huge. You see a lot of players transition directly from the U21s to the senior national team. For instance, players like Noa Natsui and others have recently earned their selections or made their debuts for the senior squad.
When you get to the U21s, the connection to the senior team gets much closer, and you feel that immediately. The entire environment feels like a senior national team rather than a youth team—everything from the travel arrangements to the hotels is a step up in quality. Being in that environment was a massive experience for me because it makes you realize just how close you are to the top, which really fuels your desire to achieve it. But even when you get there, there is still a lot of hard work left to do.

 Danish Academy Structure & Tactical Development
Josh: A really popular conversation right now within the United States is how we look at changing our own player development. There's a lot of debate over here about when to introduce 11v11. Do you start them super young, or do you wait until they are older? What age does things start getting serious in Denmark? When do they introduce the larger sides of the game and deeper tactical concepts?

Marcus Mccoy: From under-11 to under-13, you start transitioning to the full-size goals and playing 11v11. From that time on, they prepare you for the real game and start focusing on the larger side of the matches and more tactical stuff. For me, coming through the academy at Odense, the heavy tactical stuff really comes a lot when you move into the under-15 level. Academy football in Denmark has three big national leagues that start at under-15: you have the U15 league—which is the highest level in Denmark—and then the U17 league and U19 league. Below those, we have the U15 division, U17 division, and U19 division.

Josh: So those leagues are essentially the pinnacle of youth development in the country.

Marcus Mccoy: Exactly. From U15 to U17, it's a careful balance of developing individual players and preparing them for the U19 level. But once you hit U19, it’s much more like senior football—it becomes strictly about the necessity of winning. When players are young, they need to focus on mastering technical skills like dribbling and passing. As they get older, they naturally apply those sharp tools to large-sided games on the biggest pitches. It’s the natural way to build a winning mentality. 


Navigating the Dual-National Choice
Josh: Looking at your career in the medium to long term, senior international football is bound to become a major factor. It’s a massive decision, and some players feel a single, absolute connection to the country they grew up in while others look at the sporting pathway. How do you balance the emotional side of that choice?
Marcus Mccoy: We haven't concluded anything yet; we’ve just spoken about the opportunity, what we would do if it comes, and it really is a hard choice. To be honest, I’m just glad I've made it to a point where people are even speaking about this opportunity, because it means I’m in the right place and performing well at the moment.
You definitely have to think about your emotions and your heart. Some people feel like they simply cannot play for any country other than the one they grew up in because they feel so deeply connected to it. Now, I'm not saying I'm not connected to Denmark—I've lived here for 20 years now, so of course I am. But I haven't closed my mind or my heart. I feel connected both ways. In some way, I still feel a bond with the States, even though I've never been there, because I have a whole family over there. It's a complex topic to navigate.
