As an African American sports fan, I’ve seen no shortage of representation at the top when it comes to the All-American superstar (Michael Jordan, Muhammad Ali, Tiger Woods, etc.) However, when it comes to soccer, we have not yet achieved the same level of success as other sports, such as basketball or football. This is not due to any innate physical limitations or deficiencies, as some misguided people may suggest. Rather, it is the result of a complex mix of cultural, historical, and systemic factors that have hindered our progress in this sport.

First and foremost, we must acknowledge the legacy of racism in America and how it has affected the development of soccer in our communities. For decades, soccer was viewed as a "foreign" sport, associated with European and Latin American cultures. As a result, it was not widely played or supported in African American communities, which tended to focus on sports such as basketball and football that were seen as more "American." This lack of exposure and investment has contributed to a dearth of resources, infrastructure, and talent development programs that are essential for nurturing world-class soccer players.

Moreover, the perception that soccer is a "white" sport has deterred many young African Americans from pursuing it seriously. This mentality is not only damaging and flawed, but it also ignores the numerous black soccer legends who have achieved unparalleled success on the world stage. Pele, Ronaldo Nazario, Ronaldinho, George Weah, and Thierry Henry are just a few examples of black soccer icons who have dominated the sport with their skill, speed, and agility. These athletes have proven that the physical attributes commonly associated with African Americans, such as strength and explosiveness, can be leveraged to excel in soccer as well.

To change the narrative and increase the representation of African Americans in soccer, we need more role models and ambassadors who can inspire and mentor the next generation. Weston McKennie and Timothy Weah are two young, charismatic African American soccer stars who have the potential to make a significant impact in this regard. Both players are already making waves at top European clubs, and their success can help dispel the myth that black athletes are not suited for soccer. By showcasing their skills and passion for the game, they can encourage more African American youth to take up soccer and pursue their dreams.

It's important to recognize the diversity of body types among successful soccer players, and how the new crop of black USMNT stars can challenge conventional wisdom about what makes an ideal soccer player physique. Kids in the African-American communities who were told they were too short, stocky, or small to be athletes now can look no further than our national team. Mckennie for all his famed physicality and power, is every bit stout as strong, and could be seen as overweight if not for how effortlessly he dominates the midfield and leaves it all on the pitch for 90 minutes. Fellow USMNT and Leeds midfielder Tyler Adams is another young African American soccer star, but you’d never know it just by meeting him. Standing at only 5'9, Adams hardly looks like a traditional American athlete with a small, lean frame that perplexes players who often end up tackled or flat out bodied by the Leeds midfielder. Even someone with a more promoted body size like Tim Weah standing at 6'0 could have easily been encouraged to pursue track and field, or something more popular, but his size not only helps support his soccer ability, but dovetails nicely with his other obvious assets such as technicality and tactical acumen.

Of course, addressing the structural barriers that limit our access and opportunities in soccer will require a sustained and concerted effort from all stakeholders. This includes investment in grassroots programs, coaching and mentorship programs, and support for black-owned soccer businesses and initiatives. It also requires a cultural shift that values and celebrates diversity in soccer and recognizes the contributions of black athletes to the sport.

In conclusion, African American communities have the talent, passion, and potential to dominate soccer just as we have in other sports. By challenging the myths and stereotypes that have held us back, supporting our young soccer stars, and investing in our future, we can pave the way for a more inclusive and equitable soccer culture in America.
