 Why Recruiting Japanese Dual Nationals Is So Tricky for the USMNT

The United States has a deep and growing pool of talented players with Japanese heritage who are
eligible, or potentially eligible for the USMNT. Yet successfully converting that eligibility into
caps often proves far more complicated than with dual nationals from countries like Germany, England, 
or Mexico. The core issue? Japan’s strict approach to dual citizenship, combined with cultural 
identification, early commitment to Japan’s youth systems, and the personal cost some players face.

 The Citizenship Barrier
Japan does not generally allow dual citizenship for adults. Individuals who acquire Japanese nationality 
at birth (or through other means) are typically required to choose one nationality by age 22. This creates
a real or perceived hurdle for players considering a switch to the USMNT. Many dual nationals grow up in 
Japan, develop through the J.League or Japanese youth national teams, and feel deep ties to the Samurai 
Blue. Switching to the US often means formally renouncing Japanese citizenship, a heavy decision for 
players and their families.By contrast, dual nationals with German, English, or Mexican ties usually face
fewer such trade-offs. Those countries often permit (or at least do not aggressively police) dual citizenship,
and the cultural or familial pull toward the US can be stronger for players raised partly in America or with
stronger American identity. US Soccer has had notable success recruiting in those pipelines precisely because
the barriers feel lower.

 Missed Opportunities and the Zion Suzuki Case
The story of Zion Suzuki (born in New Jersey to a Japanese mother and Ghanaian father) is a cautionary tale.
Suzuki moved to Japan young, came through the Urawa Red Diamonds academy, and committed early to Japan’s youth
teams. He has since become Japan’s undisputed No. 1 goalkeeper and starred for them at the 2026 World Cup. 
Despite his American birthplace, he was never seriously pulled into the US orbit. His success highlights both
the quality of Japanese-American talent and how easily it can slip away if recruitment doesn’t start early and 
aggressively.

 Brandon Nozawa: A Genuine Opportunity
Taishi Brandon Nozawa (born in 2002 in Ginowan, Okinawa, to a Japanese mother and American father) represents a
clearer opening. The tall, athletic goalkeeper has been performing well in Belgium with Antwerp after time at
FC Tokyo. Because of his background, he holds dual nationality, and as someone born with American ties, pursuing 
the USMNT may not require the same stark choice others face. US Soccer should be sending scouts and making contact
immediately. Nozawa could help the US address its longstanding goalkeeper depth concerns and partially atone for 
the Suzuki miss.

 Other Promising Dual Nationals in the Pool
The USMNT-eligible Japanese-American pipeline isn’t limited to keepers:
Anrie Chase (born 2004 in Japan to a Japanese mother and American father of Jamaican descent) is a promising center-back 
who spent key developmental years in Texas before returning to Japan. He has broken through at a high level in Europe
(Stuttgart and later Salzburg) and has openly discussed openness to the USMNT, despite prior Japan youth caps. His 
athleticism, size, and comfort in English make him a high-upside target.


Jalen (Ren) McGhee, a young forward/attacker born on Okinawa to a Japanese mother and American father, has already
represented Japan at youth World Cup level. As a Defense Department-connected talent, he embodies the military family
overlap that often produces these dual nationals. His trajectory bears watching closely.

 The Path Forward
US Soccer’s recruitment of Japanese dual nationals requires nuance. It must respect players’ cultural realities in Japan
while clearly communicating the opportunities in the US programs earlier camps, development pathways, and a more
multicultural environment. For players like Nozawa who were born with US citizenship and have less to lose, the pitch 
should be proactive and immediate. For others already invested in Japan’s system, it may involve waiting for the right
moment or accepting that not every eligible player will switch.
