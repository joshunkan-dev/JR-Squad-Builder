 Vaughn Covil Exclusive

Josh: You are "Mr. International." Looking at your background, you've got Canadian lineage, Filipino lineage, and obviously American and English roots. Tell us about your cultural upbringing—it’s a bit of a melting pot.

Vaughn Covil: Yeah, "melting pot" is a good way to describe it. It's a bit of everything. My mom was born in the Philippines, but when she was really young, maybe one or two, her family moved to Canada. She grew up in Toronto until she started university. She eventually studied law and went to law school in California, which is where she met my dad, who was out in America from England at the time.

My middle brother and I were born in San Diego, California. That's where the American side comes from, and I grew up there until I was six or seven. Then we moved to Washington, D.C. for a year, which is really when I started to enjoy playing my football—or *soccer*, as you guys know it. After that, we moved to England back to where my dad's from, down south towards Salisbury and Southampton.

In terms of a footballing background, my dad never really played the game himself, but he absolutely loves it, which is a big reason why I'm in football. But my mom actually played for the Canada women's national team when she was in her mid-twenties. Obviously, the women's game wasn't as massive back then as it is now, but she played in college as well. Even my cousin, who is 19 and at Harvard at the moment, has been in the Canadian Women's National Team setup. So there is a fair bit of soccer pedigree in the family!


 Footballing Culture: US vs. England

Josh: How would you compare the difference in footballing culture and environment in England versus the United States?

Vaughn Covil: I think for sure there's a lot of South American influence in the States. I really noticed that during my first camp when I went over with the U.S. U-15s in Carson. A lot of the feel and the temper to the game felt very South American, alongside that dynamic athleticism. The athleticism in the States is off-the-charts crazy; you will come up against real athletes in every single position.

That said, the technical side of things is definitely getting way, way better now. Even back at U-15, I was surprised at the standard and the level of the players. I remember thinking, *"Wow, these are some really good players."* Clearly, the academy systems over there are doing their work well.

If I had to pinpoint a difference, it's more about the intensity rather than the outright quality of the players. In Europe, bigger crowds and heavier pressure naturally bring a more intense game. I never look down at any type of football—they're all different and difficult in their own right—but that sheer intensity is the standout differentiator.

Josh: That's really interesting because one thing often detailed in scouting reports about your profile is your explosiveness and intensity. Those are two major traits associated with the English game, and the Championship especially is a physically grueling league. How has that experience helped you transition from a Premier League academy like Southampton to Hull City?

Vaughn Covil: In the Championship, it's the sheer volume of games. You're never that far away from playing because there are so many matches, which means opportunities constantly arise through suspensions or injuries. Everyone is needed.

My upbringing with Southampton definitely gave me my fundamentals. They've proven for a long time that they are excellent at producing players. I had the basics down, and from there it was just about developing a mindset to adapt to different styles of play and different managers.

Right now in the game, there's a massive emphasis on pressing and being really intense off the ball. That is something I consider one of my strengths—being aggressive, winning the ball back, and just being horrible to play against. That's something a lot of English players pride themselves on, from the Premier League all the way down to non-league: first and foremost, you have to be a nightmare to play against on the pitch. You can't be nice. Off the ball is just as important, if not *more* important, than the flashy stuff on the ball like scoring goals.

Josh: Getting stuck in!

Vaughn Covil: Yeah, exactly. For lack of a better phrase!


 Rising Through the Southampton Academy

Josh: Tell us a bit more about your time at Southampton. How did they help you develop as a player, and what did you take away from that environment?

Vaughn Covil: That came about when I was around 10 or 11. I had been playing local league football and was doing really well. At the end of the season, I played a single trial game against the Southampton academy. I scored and played well, and because there were no games left in the season, they signed me almost immediately. They actually told me they don't normally move that quickly!

It was a crazy couple of weeks because that wrapped up right when I started secondary school over here—which is the equivalent of middle school in the US. It was a massive transitional year, suddenly playing for a Category 1 academy. I wasn't taking football completely seriously before that, but as soon as you step into that sort of environment, it's highly professional and you are constantly being judged.

At that time, they were just starting to build the new training ground, and you'd constantly hear about all the players who had just come through: James Ward-Prowse and Luke Shaw were playing, and Calum Chambers and Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain were right around that era. In the back of my mind, I'm thinking, *"I'm going straight to the top."* That's the aim, right?

Even though I wasn't able to break through to the first team at that specific club, the things they taught me and the experiences we had were invaluable. We traveled abroad in the summer to play tournaments against massive European clubs, and week in, week out we were playing against Chelsea and Manchester United.

It also taught me a lot off the pitch because you start living away from home. At U-11 and U-12, you have "day release" where you get out of school one day a week to get a full day of coaching at the academy, though they still hold classes there. As you get older, it turns into two or two and a half days, and eventually, you're living away from home. It forces you to grow up very quickly and look after yourself. As a young kid, you're just trying to enjoy your football, but the environment soon teaches you that your application and how you conduct yourself are absolutely key.

Josh: You mentioned some incredible players like Luke Shaw and James Ward-Prowse coming through that setup. Was there a specific player you looked up to or modeled your game after while you were there?

Vaughn Covil: Yeah. When I first arrived, I was a quick, pacey little winger who was quite dynamic and a bit different. A lot of people made comparisons between me and Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain because that's exactly how he came through the academy. Some of his former youth coaches were coaching me, so there were a lot of similarities. I looked up to him a lot and tried to base my game off what he used to do on the right wing.

I don't know if you remember Aaron Lennon, who used to play for Tottenham, but he was another one of my idols growing up. I based a lot of my early game off those two. Obviously, as my game has transitioned and I play more of a number 10 role now, I look at different players. But at that point in time, that's who I wanted to be.


 Overcoming Injury & The Sports Science of Explosiveness

Josh: So you play as a 10 now, but you spent your early career as a winger. Looking at your career so far, you had a significant ligament injury not too long ago. What was your mindset like coming back from that through all the therapy and rehab?

Vaughn Covil: There's no getting away from it—it was incredibly difficult. It was a really tough period. That was my first major injury ever; I never really used to pick up muscular issues or anything like that. So it was a massive shock just not being able to play. First of all, you can't do anything because you're on crutches, and suddenly you aren't able to do the one thing you take for granted every single day.

What it taught me most was perspective. It reminds you of what actually matters, like spending good quality time with your family. Before that, I never really had a long break to go visit them and do the normal things I missed out on while growing up in football.

It taught me not to take playing for granted—being able to step out on the pitch is a privilege. Anyone who has been injured for a few weeks or months knows how dull it is when you have to stay inside doing rehab while watching everyone else go outside to train. But I had the mindset from the get-go that I was going to come back a better athlete and a better player. It was a massive learning process about my body. You can never be 100% sure you won't injure something when you are pushing your body to its absolute limit to perform at your best, but it forces you to figure out exactly what your body needs to recover.

Josh: Let's talk sports science, because I love that you mentioned Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain as a blueprint. Stylistically, your profiles are very similar. But the similarities don't actually end with style. Because of the intense explosiveness Oxlade-Chamberlain had—especially during his Arsenal days—it made him highly susceptible to muscle and ligament injuries.

It's a common hurdle for young players who are so quick that they heavily rely on their pace. If they aren't training, eating, resting, and stretching perfectly, those muscles get incredibly irritated. It's a similar conversation people have had regarding Christian Pulisic and how he has overhauled his approach to overcome his injuries at AC Milan, becoming smarter with his running and choosing the right moments for a burst of acceleration rather than sprinting constantly. Is that a balance you've had to learn?

Vaughn Covil: Oh, for sure. Hamstring and ligament injuries are rife in the game at the moment because of the immense physical demands. As a player, you're honestly never playing at 100% physical comfort. I've looked at players I've played with and thought, *"Did you really need to sprint full speed for that ball when it was already going out of bounds?"* And then boom, they pull a hamstring.

There's an incredibly fine line between pouring your heart out on the pitch and being smart. It's hard in the heat of the moment to pause and think, *"Should I be making this run or that run?"* For me, I just try to do as much as I possibly can off the pitch to prepare myself. That's why clubs have strength and conditioning staff and why there's so much research poured into prevention. All you can do is try to put the percentages in your favor, but at the end of the day, injuries are just part of a contact sport when you are pivoting and exploding constantly. It was a learning curve to realize, *"Right, next time my body needs X, Y, and Z rather than doing this."*


 The Dual-National Question: USA, England, or Canada?

Josh: Speaking of performing at your best, American fans are incredibly eager to see you. When it comes to international football, one thing that always makes a player highly discussed is dual nationality. It's a concept American fans are very familiar with, but "dual nationality" isn't even entirely accurate for you because you are eligible to represent multiple countries. Given your family's North American sports pedigree, has representing Canada ever crossed your mind, or have they ever reached out to you?

Vaughn Covil: Canada, not so much. They haven't ever been in direct contact with me, and I haven't reached out the other way either. But I know there's a lot of talk online. My mom actively sees stuff on Twitter and constantly sends it to me like, *"Look, they want you to play for Canada!"* and I'm just like, *"Mom, chill, relax."* (laughs)

When I was U-15, I went to my first England camp, which was an incredible experience. In that exact same age group were guys like Jude Bellingham and Harvey Elliott, so there were some obviously talented players. Then the following year, I went to my first American camp out in Carson, California. So I've had a literal taste of both environments, and both are intensely competitive groups. You quickly realize how difficult it is to get into and stay in a national team.

It is definitely a frequent conversation at Christmas dinner, though! My family will ask, *"If they both called you up at the exact same time, what would you do?"* I always give the diplomatic answer: I would say yes to England to please my dad, and say yes to America to please my mom and myself! It's a loaded question because who really knows until the script is written? But I had a great experience with the Pan American team, and that brought me a lot closer to the American guys and connected me to that side of my identity. I'm definitely swaying towards America, for sure.

 Fan Questions from Twitter

Josh: We grabbed a few questions from fans online. The first one comes from Twitter user `@ColumbusOne`, who asks: *Do you have any pre-game rituals or superstitions?*

Vaughn Covil: It's funny because people always ask that. My biggest superstition is actually having *none*. Going into a game, that's the biggest thing for me. When I was younger, I used to put my left sock on before my right and all that sort of stuff. Then there was one time I forgot to do it, and I felt completely off the whole game—I hated that feeling. From then on, I told myself I'm just going to go into games and let it flow. The more you obsess over those things, the more they take over your mind. I prefer a completely blank, comfortable, and confident mind.

Josh: The next question is from `@SergeantBDub`: *At what age did you fully commit to transitioning from a winger to a midfielder?*

Vaughn Covil: For me, that transition has really happened over the last two or three seasons. It wasn't necessarily out of me actively demanding to play there; I just got placed there in a couple of training sessions and games and really enjoyed it. Even when I play on the wing, my natural tendency is to drift underneath, roll inside, and pick up those central number 10 pockets anyway.

I think coaches recognized that it was a natural evolution for my profile. I love to make inverted runs off the shoulders of the strikers and exploit the channels. I can still play on the wing and I still enjoy it when called upon, but over the last two or three years, I've slowly evolved into a central midfielder.


 Long-Term Career Horizon

Josh: Looking long-term at your career over the next decade, what are the primary goals you've set for yourself?

Vaughn Covil: My main goal has always been to play at the absolute highest level I can, for as long as I can. Striving for the top naturally forces me to improve every single facet of my game. If I reach my ultimate goals, brilliant. If I fall just short, I'll still land at a very high level playing great football. You have to shoot as high as possible.

Josh: Playing in the United States has mostly been reserved for youth national team camps for you so far, but could you see yourself moving to Major League Soccer later in your career?

Vaughn Covil: Yeah, for sure. Especially now that there's a San Diego team in the league, which obviously hits close to home for me since I was born there.

The way the league is progressing is incredible. People don't look at it as a "retirement league" anymore; it's taken very seriously on the global market. At some point in my career, I definitely want to go play in America—both for the football and the lifestyle, just to feel like an American again. And, of course, to finally catch an NFL or NBA game live!
